What is IceCube?

The IceCube Neutrino Observatory is a particle detector run by a global collaboration of over 300 scientists. It has a volume of 1 cubic kilometer of ice and is located more than a mile below the surface of the Antarctic ice sheet. To learn more, visit IceCube's website below.

Research

My research is based on leveraging the unique properties of neutrinos to probe for signals of new physical phenomena. With data from the IceCube Neutrino Observatory and the latest statistical analysis tools, I have been able to set the worldwide best limits on two types of proposed new physics: neutrino nonstandard interactions (NSI) and neutrino oscillation decoherence. The publication I authored for my NSI results was selected as a Physical Review Letters Editors' Suggestion, and my publication of the decoherence results is anticipated for late 2023.

What are Neutrinos?

Neutrinos are the least-understood fundamental particles found in nature. Electrically-neutral and less than a millionth of the size of an electron, neutrinos are extremely difficult to detect as they interact very rarely with other matter. This has given them the popular nickname of "ghost particles". In addition, neutrinos have the unique property of changing types as they move through space. These special properties, albeit difficult to work with, make neutrinos prime candidates for studies searching for new physics. My research group, the neutrino Rare Event Searches group (νRES) at the University of Texas at Arlington, focuses entirely on these searches for new physics through studying neutrinos.
